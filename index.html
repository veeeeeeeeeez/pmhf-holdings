PMHF Employee Handbook

Prediction-market hedge fund

PMHF Holdings

We trade event contracts by finding prices that look wrong. Then we bet on being right.

Overview

The Short Version

We pool investor capital into one fund and trade prediction markets. Our edge is better research, faster updates, and strict risk controls. We charge 1 and 30 — 1% management fee, 30% performance fee with a high-water mark so nobody pays us twice for recovering from our own mistakes.

We make money when our forecasts are more accurate than the market's. That's it. There's no secret. The secret is doing the boring work better than everyone else, every single day.

Mission

Why We Exist

Turn better forecasting into consistent returns. Trade responsibly. Improve price discovery in prediction markets.

That last part matters. Prediction markets work better when smart participants push prices toward truth. We think we can make money and make these markets more accurate. If we're wrong about that, we'll at least make money.

Values

Three Things We Actually Care About

We have three core values. Not seven. Not twelve. Three. Because if you have twelve values, you have zero.

01

Transparency

Prediction markets are confusing to outsiders. Most of our investors won't fully understand every trade we make. That's fine — but they should never feel like we're hiding something. We over-communicate. We explain our rationale. If you can't explain a position to an LP in plain English, you don't understand it well enough to trade it.

02

Expertise

Our returns come from better research, not better branding. The "product" at this fund is the quality of our thinking. That means we hire people who are genuinely obsessed with being right — not people who are obsessed with looking right. If you're optimizing for optics, you're at the wrong fund.

03

Risk Aversion

This sounds boring. It is boring. That's the point. No single trade should threaten the fund's survival. We are not here for glory bets. We are here to compound returns over years, not to have one incredible quarter followed by a blowup. If you want to YOLO, go trade your own money.

Operating Principles

How We Work

These are the rules we actually follow day-to-day. Not aspirational posters on a wall — operational defaults that shape how we make decisions.

Avoid risks that put company survival at stake

To make our aforementioned principle more punchy and less concrete, we believe that generally avoiding any sources of risk that put our company in jeopardy is a much more reliable guiding principle than surviving being wrong. Even when we can survive being wrong, it may place the reputation of our fund in a difficult spot, and avoiding this altogether is optimal for our success.

Investor happiness is more important than your bonus

This principle is meant to not only be guiding to our executive committee but to all our employees, traders, and anyone else involved in our success. Prioritizing profit may seem like the overarching goal of any investment firm, but any action that upsets our investors and those that give us business is a clear violation of our mission.

Audit trails are a learning opportunity, not an organizational failure

In any venture, there are always legal obligations that come as the less exciting aspects of running a corporation. We want to change this mindset from seeing, specifically in our field, audits not as a result of sloppy accounting, but instead a learning opportunity to better optimize our system of operations and demonstrate to everyone the realities of running a hedge fund.

Respond quickly to surroundings, not in pursuit of recognition

We want to demonstrate our motivation clearly: not that we are publicity obsessed and driven by recognition, but instead that we respond to our surroundings and update our information in an industry so fast paced and noisy. If we can move silently and generate reliable returns, we will always prefer this over aggressive and media obsessed actions.

Move fast in research, but only trade with evidence

In maintaining the same wording as before, we want to make a clear distinction that research is different from evidence. Research is detective work, evidence is making the arrest. This principle overall enforces what we previously stated: we encourage fast idea generation and rapid testing, but we only allocate real capital when we have clear evidence, a written thesis, and defined conditions for being wrong.

We earn bigger bets; we don't start with them

Conviction should be earned, not declared. When uncertainty is high, we start with small positions to learn from real price movement and new information. We scale only when evidence strengthens and the thesis is still holding. The tradeoff is we may leave money on the table early if the market moves fast. We accept that because protecting the fund and compounding over time matters more than one fast win.

Compensate based on impact, not titles

In the inscribed scenario, there is a key discrepancy between the title and merit of our lead engineer and their respective compensation base. When the opportunity presented itself, we knew it was time to take a fair assessment of the engineer's skillset and value their potential impact to our company in the future.

Trade short-term security for long-term precedents

When starting a business, no historical founder will ever tell you that they played it safe and moved slowly. Every founder moved fast, broke things, and ultimately took extensive risks to demonstrate that they were all in for their venture. This is the attitude we want to exemplify: the path to success requires demonstration of commitment to your goals and simultaneously setting precedents for yourself and your employees.

A sharing of risks requires a sharing of rewards

In full commitment to our traders and employees, it is crucial we demonstrate unremittable loyalty to those who help support our vision and engage in our risks. No hedge fund is ever risk free; in instituting our investment vision and strategy, internally we will reward all those who share pieces of the pie.

Management

How We Lead

These came out of hard conversations about what happens when things go wrong. Not theoretical — we stress-tested these in real crisis scenarios.

01

Survival Comes First

This principle came from the tension between Vincent wanting to protect morale, Tom focusing on the numbers, and Ilan focusing on the investment. The discussion made it clear that if the company runs out of cash, every other value becomes irrelevant. At Northstar this means decisions start with runway and survival first. It is most relevant in crisis situations where delaying hard choices could kill the company.

02

Leaders Share the Consequences

The conversation repeatedly came back to fairness and credibility. If leadership asks employees to sacrifice while protecting their own pay or perks, trust breaks quickly. This principle means executives should take visible responsibility when things go wrong, including pay cuts or reduced benefits. At Northstar it reinforces that leadership is accountable for the company's situation and must earn the trust of the team.

03

Decide and Commit

This principle came from Tom pushing for a decisive reset while Vincent was worried about making cuts that were too aggressive. The discussion showed that slow or half decisions can sometimes be worse than a tough but clear choice. For Northstar this means acting quickly once the situation is clear, instead of delaying action and letting uncertainty hurt morale, productivity, and trust across the company.

Culture

What It's Like Here

We're a small team. There's no HR department to mediate your feelings. If something's wrong, say it directly. If you disagree with a trade thesis, say it in the room — not after the meeting. If you made a mistake, own it before someone else finds it.

Curious You read about things that aren't your job
Fast You update your views quickly when evidence changes
Honest You say the hard thing early, with respect
Low-ego You'd rather be right eventually than right immediately
Resilient You take a loss, learn from it, and show up the next day

We don't value people who optimize for looking smart in meetings, who hoard information, or who treat their forecasts like personal brands.

Final Note

Read This Last

This handbook isn't a legal document. It's a statement of how we think and what we expect. If you read this and thought "these people take themselves too seriously" — fair, maybe. But prediction markets are zero-sum, the edge is thin, and the teams that win are the ones that are honest about what they're doing and why.

If you read this and thought "this is how I already think" — we should talk.
