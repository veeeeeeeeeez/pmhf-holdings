PMHF Holdings

The Short Version

We pool investor capital into one fund and trade prediction markets. Our edge is better research, faster updates, and strict risk controls. We charge 2 and 20 — 2% management fee, 20% performance fee with a high-water mark so nobody pays us twice for recovering from our own mistakes.

We make money when our forecasts are more accurate than the market's. That's it. There's no secret. The secret is doing the boring work better than everyone else, every single day.

Why We Exist

Turn better forecasting into consistent returns. Trade responsibly. Improve price discovery in prediction markets.

That last part matters. Prediction markets work better when smart participants push prices toward truth. We think we can make money and make these markets more accurate. If we're wrong about that, we'll at least make money.

Three Things We Actually Care About

We have three core values. Not seven. Not twelve. Three. Because if you have twelve values, you have zero.

01

Transparency

Prediction markets are confusing to outsiders. Most of our investors won't fully understand every trade we make. That's fine — but they should never feel like we're hiding something. We over-communicate. We explain our rationale. If you can't explain a position to an LP in plain English, you don't understand it well enough to trade it.

02

Expertise

Our returns come from better research, not better branding. The "product" at this fund is the quality of our thinking. That means we hire people who are genuinely obsessed with being right — not people who are obsessed with looking right. If you're optimizing for optics, you're at the wrong fund.

03

Risk Aversion

This sounds boring. It is boring. That's the point. No single trade should threaten the fund's survival. We are not here for glory bets. We are here to compound returns over years, not to have one incredible quarter followed by a blowup. If you want to YOLO, go trade your own money.

How We Work

These are the rules we actually follow day-to-day. Not aspirational posters on a wall — operational defaults that shape how we make decisions.

No Ego in Probabilities

Your beliefs are not your identity. If new data shows up and it contradicts your thesis, you update. You don't "defend your view." You change your mind, fast, and you move on. The market does not care about your feelings, and neither does the P&L.

The fastest way to lose money in prediction markets is to confuse being wrong with being attacked.

Test Small, Then Scale

When you're unsure — and you should be unsure often — start with a small position. See what happens. If the evidence holds, scale up. If it doesn't, you lost almost nothing and learned something.

We do not do "high conviction, size it up" on untested ideas. Earn your conviction with real market feedback.

Write the Thesis Before the Trade

Before any trade, you write down: what you think will happen, why, and what would change your mind. This is not optional. It stops emotional trading and forces you to think before you act.

If you cannot articulate the thesis clearly in writing, you don't have a thesis. You have a vibe. We don't trade vibes.

Risk-Averse Decision Making

Every position gets sized relative to expected value and downside, not upside fantasy. We hedge. We set limits. We respect stop-losses. Speed and creativity operate inside risk guardrails, not outside them.

The fund survives first. Everything else is second.

Creative Adhocracy

Our edge depends on finding mispricings faster than everyone else. That requires experimentation, rapid iteration, and a willingness to try things that might not work. If a research idea is promising, we test it. If a tool would help, we build it.

Move fast — but inside the risk limits.

Alignment of Incentives

We win when our investors win. The high-water mark means we eat our losses before we earn again. If we're not generating real returns, we don't get paid performance fees.

That's the deal. It keeps us honest.

Accountability to Investors

When we lose money — and we will lose money — we explain what happened, why it happened, and what changes. No vague hand-waving. No "markets were volatile." Specific answers. This is harder than celebrating wins, but it's more important. The funds that survive long-term are the ones that don't hide from bad quarters.

How We Lead

These came out of hard conversations about what happens when things go wrong. Not theoretical — we stress-tested these in real crisis scenarios.

Survival Comes First

When the fund's survival is at risk, we make the decision that gives us the highest probability of staying alive. Good intentions are worthless if the company runs out of cash. Every other value depends on the fund existing.

Leaders Bleed First

If we're asking the team to sacrifice — pay cuts, longer hours, or layoffs — leadership takes the hit first and visibly. No executive sits comfortably while the team absorbs pain. If you wouldn't accept the sacrifice yourself, don't ask someone else to.

Decide and Commit

In a crisis, ambiguity is more damaging than a tough call. We make one clear decision and commit to it. We do not drag the team through weeks of "we're still figuring it out." That kills morale faster than any layoff.

What It's Like Here

We're a small team. There's no HR department to mediate your feelings. If something's wrong, say it directly. If you disagree with a trade thesis, say it in the room — not after the meeting. If you made a mistake, own it before someone else finds it.

Curious you read about things that aren't your job
Fast you update your views quickly when evidence changes
Honest you say the hard thing early, with respect
Low-ego you'd rather be right eventually than right immediately
Resilient you can take a loss, learn from it, and show up the next day

We don't value people who optimize for looking smart in meetings, who hoard information, or who treat their forecasts like personal brands.

Read This Last

This handbook isn't a legal document. It's a statement of how we think and what we expect. If you read this and thought "these people take themselves too seriously" — fair, maybe. But prediction markets are zero-sum, the edge is thin, and the teams that win are the ones that are honest about what they're doing and why.

If you read this and thought "this is how I already think" — we should talk.
